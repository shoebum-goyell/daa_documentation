Kirkpatrick-Seidel Algorithm: A Detailed Overview

The Kirkpatrick-Seidel algorithm, renowned for its efficiency in computing the convex hull of a set of points in the plane, stands out for its worst-case time complexity, which aligns with the lower bound for comparison-based convex hull algorithms. This document delves into a comprehensive description of the algorithm's operation, emphasizing its divide-and-conquer strategy and the use of the median-of-medians for efficient selection.

Fundamental Concepts

Before exploring the algorithm's intricacies, it's crucial to understand several key concepts central to its methodology:

  • Convex Hull: The smallest convex polygon that encompasses all points in a given set.
  • Upper and Lower Hulls: The segments of the convex hull that lie above and below a line connecting the points with the minimum and maximum x-coordinates, respectively.
  • Median Line: A vertical line through the median x-coordinate, effectively dividing the point set into two roughly equal halves.

Overview of the Algorithm

The Kirkpatrick-Seidel algorithm employs a divide-and-conquer approach, partitioning the problem into smaller, manageable subproblems, solving each independently, and then merging the results. A notable feature of this algorithm is its process for identifying the "upper bridge" and "lower bridge" at each division, which are line segments that belong to the convex hull and span the divide created by the median line.

Algorithmic Steps

  1. Preprocessing: Sort all points by their x-coordinates and identify extreme points (pumin, pumax for the upper hull and plmin, plmax for the lower hull), which are assured parts of the convex hull.
  2. Divide: Use the median-of-medians algorithm to efficiently locate the median x-coordinate, and draw a vertical line here to split the set into two halves.
  3. Conquer: For each half, recursively identify the upper and lower bridges. This involves pairing points, calculating the median slope among these pairs, and using it to narrow down a candidate set for the bridge. Special consideration is given to vertical lines and ensuring accurate selection of the bridge segments.
  4. Merge: The upper and lower hulls are constructed by connecting the bridges identified at each step. Since the algorithm operates recursively, the merging phase is implicit in the final step that returns the complete convex hull.
  5. Postprocessing: The algorithm ensures no duplicate points are part of the final convex hull by removing consecutive duplicates from the list representing the hull.

Key Innovations and Efficiency

The Kirkpatrick-Seidel algorithm's efficiency is derived from several key innovations:

  • The median-of-medians selection ensures that the median line is determined in linear time, sidestepping the need for sorting at each division.
  • Efficient bridge finding techniques focus on slope analysis to streamline the search for bridge candidates, reducing unnecessary comparisons.
  • The divide-and-conquer strategy significantly reduces the problem size at each recursion, making the algorithm highly efficient.
  • Careful handling of special cases, such as vertical lines and points precisely on the median line, prevents potential errors and inefficiencies.

Conclusion

Through its innovative approach and meticulous attention to detail, the Kirkpatrick-Seidel algorithm represents a masterful application of computational geometry techniques, offering optimal performance in convex hull computations. Its strategic methodology and efficiency make it a foundational method in the field.
